1(a) Transformations: $f(x)=2(x-2)^4+1$

Parent function: $y=x^4$.

  1. $(x-2)$ means shift right by $2$.
  2. Multiply by $2$ means vertical stretch by factor $2$.
  3. $+1$ means shift upward by $1$.

Vertex: $(2,1)$, axis of symmetry: $x=2$.

Domain: $(-\infty,\infty)$, range: $[1,\infty)$.

Side note: Because the exponent is even, both ends go upward.

Final answer: Graph is $y=x^4$ shifted right $2$, stretched by $2$, shifted up $1$.

1(b) Zero behavior and end behavior

Given $f(x)=2(x+3)(x^2+4)^3$.

Real x-intercepts come from real zeros only.

  • $x+3=0 \Rightarrow x=-3$
  • $x^2+4=0 \Rightarrow x=\pm 2i$ (not real)

So the only real x-intercept is $(-3,0)$, multiplicity $1$.

Therefore, the graph crosses the x-axis at $x=-3$.

Leading term is $2x^7$, so end behavior resembles $y=2x^7$.

Thus: $x\to\infty \Rightarrow f(x)\to\infty$, and $x\to-\infty \Rightarrow f(x)\to-\infty$.

Side note: odd degree + positive leading coefficient means left tail down, right tail up.

Final answer: only real intercept at $x=-3$ (crossing); end behavior like $y=2x^7$.

2) Asymptotes of $f(x)=\dfrac{x^3-1}{x^2-5x-14}$

Factor denominator: $x^2-5x-14=(x-7)(x+2)$.

Factor numerator: $x^3-1=(x-1)(x^2+x+1)$.

No common factor, so vertical asymptotes are $x=7$ and $x=-2$.

Since degree difference is $1$, there is a slant asymptote.

Division gives: $$\frac{x^3-1}{x^2-5x-14}=x+5+\frac{39x+69}{x^2-5x-14}$$ so oblique asymptote is $y=x+5$.

Side note: No horizontal asymptote when numerator degree exceeds denominator by 1.

Final answer: VA: $x=7,-2$; HA: none; oblique: $y=x+5$.

3) Analyze $f(x)=\dfrac{x^2}{x^2+x-6}$

Rewrite denominator: $x^2+x-6=(x+3)(x-2)$.

Core features

  • Domain: $x\neq -3,2$.
  • Intercepts: $(0,0)$ (both x- and y-intercept).
  • Vertical asymptotes: $x=-3$, $x=2$.
  • Horizontal asymptote: $y=1$ (equal degrees).

Crossing horizontal asymptote: $$\frac{x^2}{x^2+x-6}=1\Rightarrow x=6,$$ so crossing point is $(6,1)$.

Side note: zero at $x=0$ has multiplicity 2, so the graph touches the x-axis and turns.

Final answer: Domain $\mathbb R\setminus\{-3,2\}$, intercept $(0,0)$, VA at $x=-3,2$, HA $y=1$.

4) Solve $\dfrac{5}{x-3}\le\dfrac{3}{x+1}$

$$\frac{5}{x-3}-\frac{3}{x+1}\le0$$ $$\frac{5(x+1)-3(x-3)}{(x-3)(x+1)}\le0$$ $$\frac{x+7}{(x-3)(x+1)}\le0$$

Critical values: $x=-7$ (zero), $x=-1,3$ (undefined).

Sign chart gives solution on $(-\infty,-7]$ and $(-1,3)$.

Side note: points making denominator zero are always excluded.

Final answer: $\boxed{(-\infty,-7]\cup(-1,3)}$.

5) Real zeros of $f(x)=2x^4+x^3-7x^2-3x+3$

Rational candidates: $\pm1,\pm3,\pm\frac12,\pm\frac32$.

Testing shows $x=-1$ and $x=\frac12$ are zeros.

Complete factorization: $$f(x)=(x+1)(2x-1)(x^2-3).$$

From $x^2-3=0$, remaining zeros are $x=\pm\sqrt3$.

Side note: Rational Zeros Theorem gives rational candidates; irrational zeros can still appear from leftover quadratic factors.

Final answer: $\boxed{x=-1,\;x=\frac12,\;x=\pm\sqrt3}$.

6(a) Solve $\log_3(x+1)+\log_3(x+4)=2$

Domain: $x>-1$.

$$\log_3\big((x+1)(x+4)\big)=2$$ $$x^2+5x-5=0$$ $$x=\frac{-5\pm3\sqrt5}{2}$$

Only $x=\dfrac{-5+3\sqrt5}{2}$ satisfies domain $x>-1$.

Final answer: $\boxed{x=\dfrac{-5+3\sqrt5}{2}}$.

6(b) Solve $25^x-8\cdot5^x=-16$

Let $y=5^x$, then $25^x=5^{2x}=y^2$.

$$y^2-8y+16=0$$ $$(y-4)^2=0\Rightarrow y=4$$ $$5^x=4\Rightarrow x=\log_5 4\approx0.8614$$

Side note: this is a standard quadratic-in-$5^x$ pattern.

Final answer: $\boxed{x=\log_5 4\approx0.8614}$.
